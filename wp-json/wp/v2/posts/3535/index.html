The interview below is a part of a series on New Secondary Division Courses for 2012<\/a>. Laura Shefler discusses her upcoming course, Art History<\/em>. Read the course description here.<\/a><\/p><\/blockquote>\n

1. How did you become interested in art and art teaching?<\/strong><\/p>\n

I have been fascinated by art for as long as I can remember.\u00a0 Most of us are, as young children.\u00a0 In preschool, we linger over picture books or compete for the crayons in the basket.\u00a0 I teach to help people remember<\/span> why they should care about art.\u00a0 It is something that belongs to all of us, part of our shared human history as far back as the cave painters and probably much further.<\/p>\n

2. Do you expect students to have an art background before taking this class? What other hobbies or academic interests might indicate a good fit for Art History?<\/strong><\/p>\n

Students do not need any special background in art in order to take this course.\u00a0 Those who are well informed about aspects of art history will expand upon their knowledge.\u00a0 Those who are new to the topic will come in with a different set of advantages: fresh eyes and an open mind.<\/p>\n

The study of art and architecture appeals to many other interests. Most clearly, it has a lot to offer to history buffs.\u00a0 If you want to know how Charlemagne transformed himself from Frankish King to Holy Roman Emperor, take a look at the magnificent marble chapel he built for himself at Aachen.\u00a0 At the same time, looking at art can make the past more interesting even for those who don\u2019t love history.\u00a0 If you think that the Battle of Hastings is just a boring set of dates and names, check out the action in the Bayeux Tapestry, a 230-foot-long embroidery from 1070s that provided a precedent for the modern comic book.<\/p>\n

At the same time, art history has ties with other, more technical subjects. When you read about Shang Dynasty bronze casting methods or the role of oxidation in the manufacture of Greek pottery, you are also researching the role of science in human development.\u00a0 Sophisticated mathematical concepts define Islamic tilework as well as the kolam patterns that some Indian women paint with rice powder on the ground.<\/p>\n

3. You’ve taught AP Art History with us in the past. Will this course be useful to students hoping to pursue AP Art History in the future? How else will the study of Art History aid students’ future studies?<\/strong><\/p>\n

In this class students will also learn about psychology, sociology, and even economics.\u00a0 Just as importantly, they will acquire stronger writing and critical thinking skills that will enhance their academic abilities across the board. Art history is a vast topic:\u00a0 no matter how much you know about it, you will always encounter unfamiliar images and ideas. Students will emerge from this course with a powerful set of concepts and vocabulary that will help them to approach new topics at the AP level, in college and beyond.<\/p>\n
