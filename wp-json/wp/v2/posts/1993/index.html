How did Secondary Division students play their way to learning a new language?
\n<\/strong>by Junko Hosoi<\/em><\/div>\n

From Nina Gabelko:<\/strong> Junko Hosoi, long-time sensei of ATDP\u2019s First-Year Japanese and architect of our current Japanese language and culture program, continually amazes me with the joyfulness of her teaching. Her approach to instruction comes under the rubric of \u201cmulti-modal instruction,\u201d but that doesn\u2019t even scratch the surface of the scope and depth of students\u2019 classroom participation. The activity is so intense that students have no opportunity to be self-conscious; they are much too involved in counting in Japanese while performing calisthenics, singing joyous songs, playing games, entering contests, writing essays, and performing self-authored plays. \u00a0Remember, that this class meets 18 times and not 182 times, as it does during the school year.<\/em><\/p>\n

Clearly, Hosoi-sensei\u2019s expectations for her students are as high as her support of them is deep. But even with such high expectations, not infrequently, her students exceed her expectations by such a degree that she asks herself, \u201cHow\u2019d they do that?\u201d \u00a0Well, here\u2019s how the sensei explains all of it, except for the magic that ATDP students bring to the equation:<\/em><\/p>\n

What the students accomplished:<\/em><\/p>\n

By the very fist day of the class, the vast majority of the students became able to read and write all 46 primary hiragana (Japanese alphabet) with the help of online resources and guidelines in the homework packet distributed at the orientation held two weeks before the class began.<\/p>\n

After one week (3 class meetings) almost all students were able to write a 16-line original dialogue in Japanese using expressions learned during the first week.\u00a0After three weeks, students had the opportunity to introduce themselves in Japanese in a formal manner to a Japanese person in Tokyo via Skype. Students told: name, nickname, age, school and level, home location, likes, dislikes, hobby, ethnicity, and their physical characteristics. The person in Tokyo understood our students very well and asked one or two simple questions in Japanese.<\/p>\n
