Here is a grid of X\u2019s and O\u2019s. (Why is it here? There is no elaborate backstory. It simply is<\/i>.) Now, let\u2019s draw a path on top of it.<\/p>\n

<\/a><\/p>\n

When you draw the path, something curious happens.\u00a0 As you move from one box to the next box, the X\u2019s and O\u2019s switch spots.<\/p>\n

So, as you moved from the top right box to the middle box, the X and the O switched spots.<\/p>\n

<\/a><\/p>\n

Then, when you moved down, the two X\u2019s switched.<\/p>\n

<\/a><\/p>\n

Finally, the X and O at the bottom switch.<\/p>\n

<\/a><\/p>\n

Leaving you with a grid that looks like this.<\/p>\n

<\/a><\/p>\n

 <\/p>\n

You also know (no one tells you; you simply know) these rules:<\/p>\n

    \n
  1. You can only move up, down, left, and right through a grid. No diagonal movement.<\/li>\n
  2. Each grid can only have one path. No skipping boxes.<\/li>\n
  3. A path can\u2019t enter a box more than once; a path can\u2019t intersect itself.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n

     <\/p>\n

    Now, here is a series of six grids.<\/a> (Like the first grid, none of these grids have elaborate backstories either.) Your job — no one hired you, but you feel compelled by honor and duty to complete the task — is as follows: Draw a path in grid 1 that will make it look like grid 2 when you\u2019re done. Then, draw a path in grid 2 to make it looks like grid 3. And so on and so forth. Draw paths to get from grid 1 to 2, 2 to 3, and 3 to 4 for a total of 15 points. For 5 additional points, draw paths from grid 4 to 5 and 5 to 6!<\/strong><\/p>\n
